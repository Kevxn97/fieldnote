# highlighting

## Definition
Highlighting is a core function of Obsidian Web Clipper in which users mark content on web pages and save that web content directly to an Obsidian vault through a free browser extension.

## Description
In the documented material, highlighting appears as one of the primary actions supported by Obsidian Web Clipper alongside clipping full web content. It is presented both as a general capability of the extension and as a distinct feature area with its own help topic labeled **Highlighter**.

The available evidence ties highlighting specifically to browser-based capture workflows. Obsidian Web Clipper is distributed as a browser extension for Chromium-based browsers, Firefox, Safari, and Edge, so highlighting is situated in the context of working directly on web pages rather than in a separate desktop-only editor.

Highlighting is also connected to a local-first storage model. Saved content goes to the user’s Obsidian vault, and the extension states that it does not collect user data or usage metrics. In practice, that places highlighting within a capture flow aimed at preserving selected web material in a personal knowledge base.

There is some ambiguity in the term. “Highlighting” can refer broadly to marking page content, while “Highlighter” appears to be the name of a specific documented feature. The documented material confirms the existence of both senses but does not describe the detailed workflow.

## Why It Matters
Highlighting matters because it turns web reading into direct knowledge capture. Rather than only saving an entire page, users can identify and preserve specific material from what they read and send it into their vault.

It also matters for privacy and control. Because Web Clipper saves content locally and states that it does not collect user data or usage metrics, highlighting is positioned as a personal, auditable workflow rather than a cloud-mediated annotation system.

Platform coverage increases its practical importance. Since Web Clipper is available across major browser ecosystems, highlighting is not restricted to one browser family and can fit into web research habits across desktop and mobile-supported environments such as Firefox Mobile and Safari on Apple platforms.

The feature’s prominence in the help navigation suggests it is not a minor add-on. Highlighting sits alongside clipping, reader mode, interpreter features, templates, variables, filters, and logic, which indicates it is part of the broader capture and processing workflow around web content.

## Tensions And Debates
- The term is only lightly defined: it is clear that highlighting exists, but the documented material does not specify whether it captures selected text, visual regions, annotations, or some combination.
- “Highlighting” may refer either to a general action or to a named Highlighter feature, and the boundary between those meanings is not explained.
- Cross-browser availability is stated, but any differences in highlighting behavior across Chromium browsers, Firefox, Safari, and Edge are not documented here.
- The local-first privacy model is explicit, but the tradeoff between privacy and any cloud-style synchronization or analytics is not addressed.
- Highlighting is presented near reader and interpreter features, but their interaction is unclear: it is not established whether highlighting works independently, through reader mode, or with natural-language interpretation.
